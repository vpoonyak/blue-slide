# Presentation intake and Grill Me mode

Understand the presentation before planning slides. Inspect supplied materials first, then ask only unresolved questions.

## Choose the interview depth

### Lightweight intake

Use a short intake when the task is low-risk and the user has already supplied a clear brief. Ask only the missing minimum requirements.

### Full Grill Me mode

Use the full interview when:

- the user asks to be grilled;
- the goal, audience, or deliverable is ambiguous;
- the presentation is investor-facing, executive, public, persuasive, high-stakes, or expensive to produce;
- the content contains sensitive, regulated, technical, medical, legal, or financial claims;
- multiple stakeholders may disagree about success.

Ask three to five related questions per round. Do not dump the entire questionnaire at once. Follow an answer when it reveals a consequential assumption, tension, or contradiction.

## Establish the minimum brief

Do not author slides until these are known or explicitly assumed:

1. **Audience** — who will see it and what they already know.
2. **Goal** — what they should do, decide, believe, or change.
3. **Delivery** — live talk, standalone reading deck, leave-behind, or hybrid.
4. **Context** — venue, meeting, event, room, screen, and presenter.
5. **Scope** — duration, target slide count, and Q&A allowance.
6. **Content** — available sources, evidence, data, and required material.
7. **Output** — file format, aspect ratio, language, and speaker notes.
8. **Constraints** — deadline, brand rules, confidentiality, accessibility, approvals, and must-not-change items.

If the user does not know the slide count, propose a range based on delivery time, complexity, pacing, and whether the deck must stand alone. Explain the trade-off and ask for confirmation.

## Grill Me rounds

### Round 1 — outcome and success

- Why is this presentation being created now?
- What exact action or decision should follow?
- What belief or behavior should change?
- What would make the presentation successful?
- Is there a secondary objective that must not weaken the primary one?

### Round 2 — audience

- Who is the primary audience? Who else will influence the outcome?
- How many people will attend or receive the deck?
- What do they already know, believe, fear, or misunderstand?
- How skeptical, supportive, or decision-ready are they?
- What authority do they have: approve, recommend, fund, buy, learn, or comply?
- What language, cultural context, accessibility need, or technical level should shape the communication?
- What objections or questions are most likely?

### Round 3 — delivery and format

- Will the deck support a live speaker, be read alone, or do both?
- Who will present it, and how experienced are they?
- How much speaking time and Q&A time are available?
- Is there a required or preferred slide count?
- What output is needed: PowerPoint, Google Slides, PDF, outline, or another format?
- What aspect ratio, screen, room size, or remote-meeting constraint applies?
- Are speaker notes, handouts, appendix slides, or alternate versions required?

### Round 4 — narrative

- What is the audience's current **What Is** situation?
- What is missing, difficult, costly, or risky today?
- What is the real opponent: constraint, behavior, belief, system, trade-off, or competing priority?
- What **What Could Be** future should the audience be able to imagine?
- How will the recommendation move them from here to there?
- What is the final ask, next step, owner, and timing?

### Round 5 — content and proof

- What must be included, excluded, or preserved verbatim?
- Which sources are authoritative?
- Which claims need citations or verification?
- What data, charts, images, examples, testimonials, or case studies exist?
- Are assumptions, risks, limitations, alternatives, and uncertainty explicit?
- Is any material confidential, embargoed, regulated, or personally identifiable?
- What belongs in the main story, speaker notes, or appendix?

### Round 6 — visual direction

- Is BlueSlide's canonical palette appropriate, or is another brand system mandatory?
- Is there a reference deck, template, logo, image library, or icon family?
- What tone should the deck convey: institutional, urgent, optimistic, technical, premium, educational, or another quality?
- Should the visuals prioritize photographs, icons, diagrams, charts, or typography?
- Are there licensing, attribution, representation, or image-generation restrictions?

### Round 7 — production and review

- What is the real deadline, including rehearsal and review time?
- Who reviews or approves the deck?
- How many revision rounds are available?
- Which stakeholder has final decision authority?
- What existing file or version is the source of truth?
- How will success be evaluated after delivery?

## Resolve conflicts

Name conflicts before authoring. Common examples include:

- too many messages for the available time;
- a live-talk slide count paired with a standalone-reading level of detail;
- an emotional goal unsupported by credible evidence;
- a visual preference that conflicts with accessibility or brand rules;
- stakeholder demands that compete with the audience's decision needs.

Recommend a resolution and explain the trade-off. Do not silently choose between competing requirements.

## Produce the presentation brief

Summarize the intake in this compact format:

```text
Presentation:
Primary audience:
Audience knowledge and stance:
Goal / desired action:
Big Idea:
What Is:
Opponent:
What Could Be:
Bridge / recommendation:
Delivery mode and presenter:
Duration / target slide count / Q&A:
Output / aspect ratio / language:
Required content and evidence:
Visual direction:
Constraints and must-not-change items:
Deadline / reviewers / approvals:
Success criteria:
Open questions:
Assumptions:
```

Ask the user to correct material misunderstandings. When the user says to proceed without answering, record reasonable assumptions explicitly and continue unless the unresolved item creates high-stakes risk.
