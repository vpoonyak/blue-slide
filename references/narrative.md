# Four-stage narrative framework

Use this framework to turn a topic into a persuasive audience journey:

**Goal → What Is → Build the Opponent → What Could Be**

## 1. Goal — what should the audience do?

- Define the action, decision, belief change, or life change the presentation should produce.
- State the key message as the transformation intended for the audience—not as a list of what the presenter wants to say.
- Ask **Why does this matter?**
- Ask **Better for whom?** Test the benefit at the relevant level: the individual, the organization, or society.
- Make the final call to action consistent with this goal.

## 2. What Is — what is life like now?

- Describe the audience's present situation in concrete terms.
- Show what is missing, difficult, costly, risky, or unsatisfying today.
- Use only enough evidence to make the current state recognizable and credible.
- Speak from the audience's experience rather than the presenter's internal process.

## 3. Build the Opponent — what resists change?

- Reveal disadvantages or hidden costs in the current situation that the audience may not yet see.
- Identify the obstacles that make the problem difficult to solve.
- Make the opponent a condition, constraint, behavior, belief, trade-off, or system—not necessarily a person.
- Intensify the tension with truthful consequences. Never manufacture fear or exaggerate evidence.

## 4. What Could Be — how could life become better?

- Paint a specific and desirable future that becomes possible when the audience follows the recommendation.
- Show the benefit in the audience's life, work, organization, or society.
- Contrast the future with the current state so the transformation is unmistakable.
- Reconnect the future to the original key message and end with the required action.

## Build the bridge

After the audience understands the gap between the present and the possible future, answer:

**How do we get from here to there?**

- Present only the steps needed to make the action feel achievable.
- Connect each step to an opponent it overcomes.
- Keep implementation detail proportional to the audience's decision; move diligence detail to an appendix.

## Quality checks

- Can the goal be expressed as an audience action or change?
- Is “What Is” recognizable to the audience?
- Is the opponent real, specific, and honestly supported?
- Is “What Could Be” concrete enough to visualize?
- Does the bridge show a credible path rather than a leap of faith?
